# Personas — CreatorPulse India

Three primary personas drive the product: one supply-side (creator) and two demand-side (in-house brand, agency). Each persona maps to the user stories in `user_stories.md`.

---

## Anjali Menon — Rising fitness creator (supply side)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age / location** | 27 · tier-2 city (Kochi) |
| **Channel** | ~50,000 subscribers, fitness niche, ~2 years old, growing steadily |
| **Goals** | Grow subscribers, understand what's working in her niche, start landing brand deals at a fair rate |
| **Tools today** | YouTube Studio analytics, Instagram insights, gut feel, peer WhatsApp groups |
| **Frustrations** | YouTube Studio shows her own numbers but no peer context; she has no idea what a brand should pay her; she can't tell whether fitness demand is rising or fading |
| **What she needs from CreatorPulse** | Peer benchmark within her archetype, a niche-demand forecast, an engagement-quality read, and a defensible sponsored-video rate to quote brands |
| **Key stories** | CS-1, CS-2, CS-3, CS-4, CS-5, CS-6 |
| **Success looks like** | She opens her profile, sees she's above the fitness-cluster median on engagement, sees fitness demand accelerating into the new year, and quotes a brand a rate she can justify |

---

## Vikram Shah — D2C founder / in-house marketing lead (demand side)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age / location** | 34 · metro (Bengaluru) |
| **Role** | Founder of a D2C brand; runs a 10-person marketing team |
| **Budget** | ~₹40 lakh/quarter on influencer campaigns |
| **Goals** | Find fit creators fast, stop wasting budget on inflated engagement, justify spend to investors |
| **Tools today** | Spreadsheets, agency recommendations, manual Instagram/YouTube browsing |
| **Frustrations** | Discovery is slow and follower-count-driven; he's been burned by creators with purchased engagement; enterprise tools like HypeAuditor (~₹2.5L/yr) are over-built and over-priced for his team |
| **What he needs from CreatorPulse** | Brief-to-shortlist in under a minute, transparent engagement-risk screening, a cost estimate to check fit against budget, and pricing he can justify for a 10-person team |
| **Key stories** | BR-1, BR-2, BR-3, BR-4, BR-5 |
| **Success looks like** | He types a brief, gets 20 screened creators ranked with a visible breakdown, drops the two with high engagement-risk flags, and shortlists five for outreach — in one sitting |

---

## Sneha Iyer — Agency campaign planner (demand side, power user)

| | |
|---|---|
| **Age / location** | 30 · metro (Mumbai) |
| **Role** | Campaign planner at an influencer-marketing agency |
| **Volume** | ~30 brand briefs/month across multiple clients and niches |
| **Goals** | Screen and shortlist at volume, present defensible recommendations to clients, move faster than competing agencies |
| **Tools today** | An enterprise tool seat she shares with the team, plus heavy spreadsheet work |
| **Frustrations** | Seat limits and per-report costs on enterprise tools; repetitive manual screening; clients want to see *why* a creator was chosen |
| **What she needs from CreatorPulse** | High-throughput discovery, exportable shortlists, a score breakdown she can paste into client decks, and multi-niche coverage |
| **Key stories** | BR-1, BR-2, BR-3, BR-6 (and BR-5 for client-facing comparisons) |
| **Success looks like** | She runs five briefs in a morning, exports each shortlist to CSV with the score breakdown, and walks into client calls with a defensible, screened list — the multi-persona power-user cohort |

---

*These personas anchor the SWOT, gap analysis and competitive teardown: CreatorPulse wins by serving Anjali (free, supply) and Vikram/Sneha (paid, demand) from one data asset, where incumbents serve only a slice of the demand side at enterprise prices.*
